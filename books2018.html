A Year In Books

[WIP] I read a lot of good stuff this year and wanted to put together a short piece about it.

The contents in this little bit of writing encompass the things I read between May 2018 and May 2019. And because the things I think about are typically a product of the things that I read, this piece might also be considered a sketch, perhaps, of those thoughts.

Below, you'll notice that I've compiled the books I've read this year into a list (the order's arbitrary). From each book, I've selected a brief passage that I, for whatever reason, feel captures something interesting or important. Hopefully, at least one of these passages will pique your interest.

This year, change was abundant and the typical constants were in flux. I think because of this, I spent a lot of time thinking about lives - I graviated towards biographical accounts (9, 11, 12, 13) and Bildungsromans (2, 5, 7, 8). Of the latter, the Hesse novels (2, 7) were particularly special to me. There were many thematic elements which felt especially poignant - Siddhartha (2) is a story of perpetual rebirth (a nod to Hesse's love for Buddhist philosophy) - that is, the story of a single life which itself contains many. It's a story about both the fluidity of the singular self and the many selves the singular contains - some wise, some foolish, some good, and some evil - and how the potential for each of these selves is within each of us at all times, the story of their emergence and disappearance - the way in which they flow into, bear on, and shape each other - telling the story of a life.

Earlier this year, I attended a retrospective of Alexander Calder's work (14) presented by the Montreal Museum of Fine Arts. Non-contentious claim - Calder was a genius and MMFA did a brilliant job showcasing what felt like an endless number of pieces. Cut to the entrance and we're greeted by a set of sculpted wire portraits, set in front of a crisp white wall, dangling from strands of fishing line and illuminated from an angle. The lighting is set up in such a way that the wiring of the portraits eachs casts its own shadow upon the wall, collapsing three dimensions to two - lines separable in three-dimensional space are now forced to overlap and intersect each other in complex and interesting ways. In moving through spatial dimensions, we see something new emerge. And now, this begs the question: do we distinguish between these two forms? That is, are we to consider these objects distinct in their own right or are they one and the same? To my great surprise, the transformation of Calder's sculptures through spatial dimensions quite nicely mirrors the transformation of Hesse's Siddhartha through the temporal one.

On that note, The Glass Bead Game (7), was possibly my favourite read of the year. Set in a distant future, Hesse's protagonist Joseph Knecht quickly rises through the ranks of society's intellectual elite, a prodigy of Hesse's byzantine invention - the Glass Bead Game. The (alluring) premise of the GBG is the distillation of our intellectual history - music, mathematics, philosophy, linguistics, and so on - to a set of abstract symbols, a universal language via which themes and structures of these disparate branches can speak to each other. Players seek to weave these themes and structures into a intricate tapestry, synthesizing decades of scholarship into some unifying narrative, which both illuminates the hidden truths of its source materials and is too some profound truth in itself.

I found (and still find) the premise of the GBG beautiful. Thinking now, it feels remniscient of the Langlands program, possibly my favourite project in the history of mathematical research and on which my favourite essay from When Einstein Walked With Godel (11) is written.

Despite the inclination of its title, however, the novel is less about the game as it is the player.
